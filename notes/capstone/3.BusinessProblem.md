# Business Problem 1 – Customer Registration

### Scenario

A new customer visits the insurance company to buy a policy.

The executive asks:

* Customer ID
* Name
* Age
* Mobile Number
* Address

All these values belong to **one customer**, so they should be grouped together.

**Question to students:**

> "Should we create five separate variables, or is there a better way to keep all customer information together?"

This naturally leads to **Structure**.

---

# Business Problem 2 – Thousands of Customers

After one month, the company has **5,000 customers**.

Using one structure variable is no longer enough.

The company needs to:

* Add new customers
* Search customers
* Update customer details
* Delete customers
* Display all customers

**Question to students:**

> "How can we store thousands of customer records efficiently?"

This introduces an **Array of Structures**.

---

# Business Problem 3 – Premium Calculation

The premium depends on:

* Customer's age
* Policy type

Example:

| Age Group | Basic | Silver | Gold |
| --------- | ----- | ------ | ---- |
| 18–25     | 1500  | 2200   | 3500 |
| 26–40     | 2000  | 3000   | 4500 |
| 41–60     | 3000  | 4500   | 6000 |

The executive enters:

* Age = 35
* Policy = Gold

The system should immediately return the premium.

**Question to students:**

> "How can we store information that depends on both rows and columns?"

This introduces a **2D Array (Matrix)**.

---

# Business Problem 4 – Claim Registration

Every day, customers submit insurance claims.

Today:

* Claim 101
* Claim 102
* Claim 103

Tomorrow:

* Claim 104
* Claim 105

The number of claims changes every day.

**Question to students:**

> "Can we predict how many claims will arrive? Should we allocate a huge array or use a structure that grows as needed?"

This introduces a **Linked List**.

---

# Business Problem 5 – Claim Processing

Claims must be processed in the order they arrive.

Example:

* Ravi submits claim at 9:00 AM
* Amit submits claim at 9:05 AM
* Priya submits claim at 9:10 AM

Processing Priya's claim before Ravi's would be unfair.

**Question to students:**

> "Which data structure guarantees First Come, First Served?"

This introduces a **Queue (FIFO)**.

---

# Business Problem 6 – Emergency Claims

Some claims are urgent.

Examples:

* Road accident ✔
* Heart surgery ✔
* Routine reimbursement ✖

Emergency cases should be processed first, even if they arrive later.

**Question to students:**

> "How can the system give preference to urgent claims?"

This introduces a **Priority Queue**.

---

# Business Problem 7 – Undo Mistakes

An employee accidentally changes a customer's premium.

The customer complains.

The manager clicks **Undo**.

The system restores the previous data.

**Question to students:**

> "Where should we keep the previous version of the record?"

This introduces a **Stack (LIFO)**.

---

# Business Problem 8 – Fast Policy Search

The company has **10 lakh (1 million) policies**.

Searching one by one is too slow.

Customers expect instant results.

**Question to students:**

> "How can we organize policy records so searching is much faster than a linear search?"

This introduces a **Binary Search Tree**.

---

# Business Problem 9 – Customer Login

Every customer logs in using:

* Email
* Password

The company has millions of users.

Searching every email one by one is inefficient.

**Question to students:**

> "How can we almost instantly locate a user's account?"

This introduces a **Hash Table**.

---

# Business Problem 10 – Business Reports

Every evening, the manager wants:

* Highest premium collected
* Largest claim amount
* Top 10 customers

Sorting all records every time is expensive.

**Question to students:**

> "Can we keep the largest values readily available?"

This introduces a **Heap**.

---

# Business Problem 11 – Branch Network

The insurance company has branches in:

* Pune
* Mumbai
* Nashik
* Nagpur
* Aurangabad

Survey officers travel between branches to inspect claims.

The company wants to know:

* Which branches are connected?
* What is the shortest route to reach a customer?

**Question to students:**

> "How do we represent cities and the roads connecting them?"

This introduces a **Graph**.

---

## Mentor Teaching Formula

For every topic, follow the same pattern:

**Business Problem → Student Discussion → Real-Life Example → Data Structure Choice → Memory Diagram → Code Implementation → Testing → Integration into the Insurance Management System**

This approach transforms the class from **"Today we will learn Linked Lists"** into **"Today our insurance company has a new business requirement. Which data structure can solve it?"** Students begin thinking like software engineers who choose data structures to solve real business problems, rather than memorizing them in isolation.
